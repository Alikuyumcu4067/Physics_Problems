## 🌌 Microgravity at ISS Altitude

**Given:**
- ISS altitude: $h = 400\ \text{km}$
- Earth's radius: $R_E = 6378\ \text{km}$
- Surface gravity: $g_0 = 9.81\ \text{m/s}^2$

---

### 🎯 Objective

Find the gravitational acceleration at ISS altitude and explain why astronauts feel weightless.

---

### 📐 Step 1: Use the Gravitational Acceleration Formula

$$g_h = g_0 \left(\frac{R_E}{R_E + h}\right)^2$$

> - Gravity decreases with the square of the distance from Earth's center
> - It never truly reaches zero

---

### 📌 Step 2: Calculate the Ratio

$$\frac{R_E}{R_E + h} = \frac{6378}{6378 + 400} = \frac{6378}{6778} = 0.9410$$

---

### 📌 Step 3: Substitute into the Formula

$$g_h = 9.81 \times (0.9410)^2 = 9.81 \times 0.8855$$

$$\boxed{g_h \approx 8.68\ \text{m/s}^2}$$

> This is about **88.5%** of surface gravity — far from zero!

---

### 📌 Step 4: Why Do Astronauts Feel Weightless?

Despite $g_h \approx 8.68\ \text{m/s}^2$, astronauts experience weightlessness because:

- The ISS and everything inside it are in **continuous free fall** toward Earth
- The ISS moves forward fast enough to **keep missing** Earth as it falls
- Both the astronaut and the spacecraft accelerate at **exactly the same rate**
- There is **no normal (contact) force** between them → sensation of weightlessness

$$\boxed{\text{Weightlessness} \neq \text{Zero gravity} \quad \text{(it is orbital free fall)}}$$

---

### ✅ Physical Interpretation

| Location | Gravitational acceleration |
|----------|---------------------------|
| Earth's surface | $9.81\ \text{m/s}^2$ |
| ISS altitude (400 km) | $8.68\ \text{m/s}^2$ |
| Reduction | $\approx 11.5\%$ less |

- This phenomenon is called **microgravity**, not zero gravity
- An astronaut with mass 70 kg would still experience a gravitational force of **$\approx 608\ \text{N}$** at ISS altitude

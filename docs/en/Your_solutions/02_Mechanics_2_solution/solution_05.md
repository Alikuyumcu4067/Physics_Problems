## 5. Inelastic Collision (Runner and Cart)

### Problem Statement
A **70 kg** runner moving at **3 m/s** jumps onto a **140 kg** stationary cart. 
1. What is the final speed of the cart with the runner? 
2. Is kinetic energy conserved in this collision? Explain.

---

### Analytical Solution

#### Step 1: Conservation of Momentum
Since there are no external horizontal forces, the total linear momentum of the system is conserved:
$$m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = (m_1 + m_2) V_{final}$$

Given:
- Runner mass ($m_1$): $70 \, kg$
- Runner velocity ($v_1$): $3 \, m/s$
- Cart mass ($m_2$): $140 \, kg$
- Cart velocity ($v_2$): $0 \, m/s$ (stationary)

Substituting the values:
$$(70 \times 3) + (140 \times 0) = (70 + 140) \cdot V_{final}$$
$$210 = 210 \cdot V_{final}$$
$$V_{final} = \mathbf{1 \, m/s}$$

#### Step 2: Kinetic Energy Analysis
We calculate the kinetic energy ($KE$) before and after the collision to check for conservation:
- **Initial $KE$:** $$KE_i = \frac{1}{2} m_1 v_1^2 = \frac{1}{2} (70)(3^2) = 315 \, J$$
- **Final $KE$:** $$KE_f = \frac{1}{2} (m_1 + m_2) V_{final}^2 = \frac{1}{2} (210)(1^2) = 105 \, J$$

**Comparison:**
$$KE_i (315 \, J) \neq KE_f (105 \, J)$$

**Conclusion:** Kinetic energy is **NOT conserved**. This is a perfectly inelastic collision. The "lost" energy ($210 \, J$) is transformed into other forms of energy, such as internal heat, sound, or work done during the runner's impact and stabilization on the cart.

**Result:** The final speed is **1 m/s**, and kinetic energy is not conserved.

## 6. Energy Dissipation (Bouncing Ball)

### Problem Statement
A tennis ball is dropped from an initial height of **2.0 meters**. After each bounce, it loses **30%** of its mechanical energy. To what height does it rise after the **second bounce**?

---

### Analytical Solution

The mechanical energy of a ball at its maximum height is purely gravitational potential energy:
$$E = mgh$$

If the ball loses 30% of its energy, it retains **70%** (or $0.7$) of its energy after each bounce ($E_{after} = 0.7 \times E_{before}$).

#### Step 1: Energy and Height Relationship
Since $E \propto h$, losing a certain percentage of energy is equivalent to losing the same percentage of maximum height:
$$h_{new} = 0.7 \times h_{old}$$

#### Step 2: Calculation After the First Bounce
The height after the 1st bounce ($h_1$):
$$h_1 = 0.7 \times h_0$$
$$h_1 = 0.7 \times 2.0 \, m = \mathbf{1.4 \, m}$$

#### Step 3: Calculation After the Second Bounce
The height after the 2nd bounce ($h_2$):
$$h_2 = 0.7 \times h_1$$
$$h_2 = 0.7 \times 1.4 \, m = \mathbf{0.98 \, m}$$

---

### Alternative Method (Formulaic)
For $n$ bounces, the final height $h_n$ can be calculated as:
$$h_n = h_0 \times (0.7)^n$$
$$h_2 = 2.0 \times (0.7)^2 = 2.0 \times 0.49 = \mathbf{0.98 \, m}$$

**Result:** The ball rises to a height of **0.98 meters** after the second bounce.
